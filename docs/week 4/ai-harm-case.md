**AI HARM CASE**

The Epic Sepsis Model (ESM) is an artificial intelligence system widely integrated into electronic health record (EHR) networks to predict the onset of sepsis in hospitalized patients (1). In an external validation study conducted by researchers at the University of Michigan, the system’s real-world performance was evaluated on a larger clinical trial of nearly 40,000 hospitalizations (1). The clinical outcome was poor, on which the AI failed to identify 1,709 who developed sepsis, translating to a 67% failure rate (1). Conversely, the system generated a massive volume of false positives, creating an 18% false alert burden. Critical time windows for administering lifesaving intravenous antibiotics were repeatedly missed for deteriorating patients, while clinical care teams were simultaneously overwhelmed by thousands of irrelevant warnings.  
The immediate cause of the failure was due to the predictive model’s severe lack of discriminative ability and poor mathematical calibration when exposed to external, real-world patient data (1). The algorithm produced consistently inaccurate clinical risk scores and flawed confidence thresholds that did not align to the reality of the patients’ physiological deterioration. It triggered diagnostic alerts too late for patients who were actually experiencing sepsis, and it unnecessarily flagged patients who were entirely stable (1).  
This failure occurred because it was believed that a centrally trained, static proprietary model could seamlessly generalize across disparate clinical environments without local adjustments. The developers failed to anticipate the highly localized nature of healthcare data and clinical workflows. They assumed that the physiological data distributions, patient demographics, and distinct EHR coding practices present in their original training datasets would perfectly mirror those of every new hospital deploying the system (1). There was a failure to recognize that solving complex, systemic problems requires continuous calibration rather than an inflexible software deployment model.  
This incident represents both a critical technical failure and a human factor failure. The underlying algorithm was poorly calibrated for the target patient population, which directly outputted statistically invalid predictions and missing clinical events (1). From a human-factors perspective, the large volume of false positive alerts that came into the clinical interface induced alert fatigue (1). Clinicians were quickly conditioned by the workflow interface to dismiss or ignore the system’s warnings, which rendered even the accurate diagnostic alerts completely useless in practice.  
The clinical risk could have been prevented by instituting a mandatory validation phase, running the model in a test mode before full scale activation, coupled with an automated data-drift listener to monitor ongoing performance. If the implementation protocol had strictly required the system to prove its accuracy against the local hospital infrastructure and patient data before actively pushing alerts to nursing interfaces, the lack of performance from the AI would have been isolated from practice and corrected immediately (1).
